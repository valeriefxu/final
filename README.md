# final README
When I learned AP US History in my junior of highschool, I was left with a very naive concept of the United States during and post Civil War. The textbook I read taught a very simple concept: the South was defined by racial terror, slavery, and lawlessness, and the North by relative tolerance and the rule of law. With the Union's victory came the power to narrate; this project exists to challenge that narrative, insisting that history be written not by the side that won, but by the data that survived. I am from California and have lived there my entire life, which is why I find so interesting that California had such a high rate of extralegal violence (which I will dive deeper in the later paragraphs). 


The central question driving this research is whether different regions of the United States exhibited distinct patterns of extralegal collective violence between 1849 and 1865, and whether computational analysis of that violence can provide empirical evidence against the North/South moral binary that still structures popular historical memory. Using Python-based keyword classification, probabilistic modeling, natural language processing, and time-series analysis across three historical datasets, this project argues that racialized extralegal violence was not a Southern outlier but a national practice. The South exhibited a higher probability of racial violence per incident; the West and North together produced rates that cannot be explained away as marginal noise. California, admitted to the Union as a free state in 1850, recorded the highest number of racial extralegal incidents. And when the Civil War ended, the violence did not end with it; it transformed, intensified, and remained geographically distributed. This is not a new historical question because in James W. Loewen’s Lies My Teacher Told Me (1995), he explains that the framing of the north as a utopia is a national myth embedded in US high-school textbooks. This is not an essay about giving the South’s unforgivable actions leniency, but to instead shed light on underrepresenting Northern complicity. 
The first issue I addressed was a flaw in the MVP's keyword extraction. I was barely extracting any new words in the MVP because the Type field caught them already. The solution was a two-layer keyword classification system. The first layer uses a broad keyword list: including terms like "slave," "enslaved," "mexican," "negro," "colored," "chinese," and "indian", applied across both the Type and Description fields to assign each incident to either "Racial/Slavery-Related" or "Other." The second layer, a matched_keyword function, deliberately skips the generic terms "racial" and "slavery" to surface which specific group was targeted. This is a critical methodological choice: without skipping those generic terms, they absorb nearly all matches and hide the granular pattern of who was actually being killed. The result is that incidents coded as simply "racial" in the original data can be broken down by specific target group: Mexican, Black, Indigenous, and Chinese, which allowed me to discover unique analyses about California.

 In the South and Border South, approximately 78.7% of extralegal incidents were classified as racial or slavery-related. In the West and North, the percentage was 53.4%. The South had a roughly 25-percentage-point higher probability of racial violence per incident. This is a meaningful difference that we should not overlook.
 
The NLP analysis deepens this picture. The most frequent term in racial and slavery-related incident descriptions is "alleged", appearing 110 times. This finding is analytically significant in ways that a frequency count alone cannot convey. It reveals that extralegal violence across both regions operated through camouflaged legalism: perpetrators almost always justified killing through an accusation, however unverified, however impossible to contest. This logic was not Southern. It was available everywhere and applied to Mexican and Chinese miners in California to free-soilers in Kansas. The medium of violence was regional; the grammar of its justification was national. The sub-period NLP analysis adds a temporal dimension. Words unique to the 1849–1860 period cluster around "free," "mexicans," "regulators," "soil," and "election" reflecting the specific flashpoints of the decade: the struggle over free soil in western territories, the activities of vigilante regulator groups, and the intensifying politicization of slavery as a national question. By 1861–1865, the vocabulary shifts toward the war itself. Violence did not pause during the Civil War; it was reorganized by it.

The most surprising instance I discovered is California recorded more racial extralegal incidents than any other state in the dataset, including every slave state. California recorded 90 total extralegal incidents, and 78 of them were racial/slavery-related. This information challenges the notion of the North’s racial tolerance. Keyword analysis helped me understand why this was the case in California. Of California's racial incidents, at least 29 explicitly targeted Mexican residents, accounting for 38.5% of the state's racial extralegal violence. An additional 48 incidents were coded as "racial/slavery unspecified," many of which likely contain additional anti-Mexican violence. The 38.5% figure is, as the code explicitly notes, a floor rather than a ceiling. California's racial violence was not driven by slavery. It was driven by settler expansion and Gold Rush competition for land, labor, and resources. Mexican Californians, many of whom had lived in the region under Mexican sovereignty prior to the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, were dispossessed, criminalized, and killed by Anglo settlers who used both extralegal mob action and the rhetoric of lawlessness to justify it.

The probabilistic model reinforces this at the level of victim classification. Across the dataset, Mexican and Hispanic victims are consistently labeled as "alleged criminals" or "alleged murderers", a pattern visible in the top target classifications by probability. The criminalization of the victim was the mechanism through which racial violence was legitimized in the West. This differs slightly from Southern patterns, where Black victims were more directly tied to the categories of slavery and racial control without the same mediating fiction of frontier criminality. The difference in mechanism does not produce a difference in outcome: in both cases, racialized extralegal killing was normalized, widespread, and largely unpunished.

For my final project, I also wanted to extend my historical question’s time frame. Section 2 (as seen on the github) of the analysis exists to prevent that implication by including two additional datasets: a historical American lynching dataset (post-1865) and a lethal anti-Chinese violence dataset (1850–1910). This extends the analysis forward in time and across victim communities, testing whether the geographic pattern identified in the core dataset persisted after the war. The lynching time series produces a finding that should be difficult to absorb: documented lynchings increased after the Civil War ended, peaking during and after Reconstruction. The vertical reference lines in the time-series chart, marking 1865 (war ends) and 1877 (Reconstruction ends) , make visible what the numbers alone might obscure: that the withdrawal of federal enforcement after 1877 corresponded with an intensification of extralegal racial violence, not its decline. The state-level breakdown is equally important. Non-Southern states appear consistently in the top 25 lynching states: Indiana, Ohio, and others, confirming that post-war lynching was not exclusively a Southern phenomenon. The pattern identified in 1849–1865 did not evaporate at Appomattox.
The anti-Chinese violence dataset adds a third thread to this argument. Lethal violence against Chinese immigrants was overwhelmingly concentrated in the West and North: California, Wyoming, Oregon, and Washington. This is not a Southern story at all. It is the story of a different racial logic: nativism, labor competition, and the anxieties of industrializing western economies, which produced the same outcome: organized, extralegal, racialized killing. The anti-Chinese movement focused on so-called moral issues like prostitution, polygamy, and "coolie" labor, all framed as forms of slavery. The hostility frequently resulted in violence, exacerbated by cases like People v. Hall in 1854, where a white miner who killed a Chinese miner was initially convicted based on Chinese witness testimony. That conviction was later overturned on the grounds that Chinese people could not testify against white citizens, meaning Chinese victims had virtually no legal rights. The South had the slave system as its organizing framework for racial terror. The West had settler colonialism and labor competition. The mechanisms were different; the outcome, extralegal, racialized killing with no legal recourse for victims, was identical. People v. Hall made this structural: when Chinese witnesses cannot testify against white defendants, the courtroom itself becomes an extension of extralegal power. The law did not protect Chinese victims any more than it protected enslaved Black people in the South or Mexican Californians dispossessed by Gold Rush settlers. What the three datasets show, taken together, is that America did not have a Southern race problem. It had a national one, expressed through whatever racial logic was available in a given region at a given moment.
